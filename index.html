Athlone – The Birthplace of Modern Music

This unveils the vibrant musical legacy of Athlone, beginning with Patrick Keating, the legendary bandmaster who returned from the Battle of Waterloo. His students, including the influential Patrick Gilmore, transformed the global music landscape and gave Ireland a unifying voice that resonated far beyond its shores. We’ll trace the journey of John McCormack, Athlone’s beloved son, whose captivating voice enchanted millions worldwide. Through times of hardship, music became Athlone’s heartbeat, offering solace and strength to unsung heroes of Irish history. You’ll discover remarkable figures like Michael Kilkelly, Henry Foy, and the Athlone Pipers—names often overlooked but whose contributions left an indelible mark. This is the untold story of Athlone’s musical heritage—a powerful tale of creativity, resilience, and connection that demands to be heard.

Patrick Keating (1796–1875)

Revolution Through Music
[Image: Patrick Keating]

Patrick Keating was no ordinary bandmaster; he was a transformative force in Athlone’s cultural history. A veteran of the Battle of Waterloo, Keating honed his musical expertise in Naples before returning to his hometown in the 1820s, then derided as a "rotten borough" plagued by corruption and stagnation. Undeterred, he set about revitalizing Athlone’s spirit through music. By training local musicians and forming disciplined bands, Keating sparked a cultural renaissance, and by the 1840s, Athlone had gained renown for its vibrant musical scene.

His influence reached far beyond his hometown. His students, ignited by his passion and rigor, carried his legacy to the world stage. Most notably, Patrick Gilmore, one of Keating’s protégés, rose to become a towering figure in music history, celebrated for his contributions to American band music. Keating’s work laid the groundwork for a musical movement that inspired unity and resilience during times of hardship and change, leaving an indelible mark on both Athlone and the global stage.

In the summer of 1843, Keating stood at the heart of a historic moment, conducting his Athlone band at Daniel O’Connell’s colossal Monster Meeting in Summerhill. As a fervent supporter of O’Connell’s campaign for Irish self-governance, Keating brought his musical prowess to the fore, rallying thousands with stirring melodies that echoed the call for change. His band, meticulously trained and infused with patriotic zeal, performed with such power that it transformed the gathering into a symphony of unity and hope.

As Ireland staggered under the weight of the Great Famine in the late 1840s, Patrick Keating’s music became a beacon of resilience in Athlone. Amidst widespread suffering, he continued to teach, pouring his expertise into a young Patrick Gilmore. Keating’s rigorous training instilled in Gilmore not just technical skill but a profound sense of music’s power to uplift and unite.

Patrick Gilmore

A Global Music Force & Student Who Would Change the World
[Image: Patrick Gilmore]

While famine ravaged the land, Patrick Keating’s mentorship offered hope. Patrick Gilmore, under his guidance, mastered the art of band leadership, blending Irish melodies with disciplined precision. By the time Gilmore emigrated to America in 1849, he carried Keating’s legacy with him.

When the American Civil War erupted, Gilmore, already a celebrated bandmaster, enlisted with the 24th Massachusetts Volunteer Infantry, bringing music to the front lines. His band lifted soldiers’ spirits, entertained prisoners, and served as stretcher-bearers. In 1863, Gilmore penned the iconic “When Johnny Comes Marching Home.”

In June 1869, Gilmore orchestrated the National Peace Jubilee in Boston, uniting 1,000 instrumentalists and 10,000 vocalists to celebrate peace. The event's crowning moment, Verdi’s “Anvil Chorus” with 100 firemen hammering anvils, showcased his flair for showmanship. On October 28, 1886, Gilmore led his band at the dedication of the Statue of Liberty. His career included gracing the 1876 Centennial Celebration, launching New Year’s Eve in Times Square, and founding Gilmore’s Concert Garden (later Madison Square Garden), earning him the title “Father of the American Band.”

In New York, Gilmore championed Charles Stewart Parnell’s vision for Irish Home Rule. At an 1880 concert, his band performed for Parnell’s speech, raising funds for the Irish Land League and weaving nationalist themes into his programs.

Michael Kilkelly

Mentorship and Resistance & Bands as Symbols of Resistance
[Image: Michael Kilkelly]

Patrick Keating’s influence in Athlone extended beyond music to the heart of resistance. A passionate nationalist, Keating mentored Michael Kilkelly in both the art of music and the spirit of defiance against British rule. Under Keating’s guidance, Kilkelly honed his skills, later becoming a pivotal figure in Athlone’s musical and political scene. Together, they wove music into the fabric of resistance, using performances to inspire hope and unity.

In the late 1870s, as the Land War erupted, Kilkelly emerged as a musical and moral force. Leading the Athlone Brass Band, he brought stirring melodies to the front lines, performing at sites of land evictions to bolster beleaguered tenants. His band’s defiant tunes rallied communities, offering solace and solidarity. Kilkelly’s presence transformed his band into a symbol of resistance, echoing the fervor of the Irish National Land League.

Back in Athlone, Kilkelly also led the Athlone Brass Band to bolster local support for Parnell’s Land League. During the Land War’s tense eviction scenes, Kilkelly’s band played defiant Irish airs, uplifting tenants. In the early 1890s, as choirmaster at St. Peter’s Church, he discovered young John McCormack, nurturing his extraordinary gift.

Henry Foy

New Rhythms in Athlone & The Birth of Irish Jazz
[Image: Henry Foy]

In 1902, Henry “Harry” Foy, a spirited 21-year-old music professor, co-founded the Athlone Musical Society, ushering in a vibrant era. The society staged its first operetta, *The Pirates of Penzance*, in 1907, marking the start of a legacy that would endure for over a century, becoming Ireland’s oldest continuously active musical society.

In 1919, Foy launched Ireland’s first jazz band, the Harry Foy Jazz Quartette. Inspired by the Gaelic League and the global jazz craze, his quartet blended syncopated rhythms with Irish melodies. Foy embraced jazz as a modern expression of cultural defiance, resonating with Ireland’s growing nationalist spirit during the War of Independence. His quartet introduced Athlone to jazz’s lively rhythms, challenging traditional norms.

Count John McCormack

A Voice Discovered & The World’s Greatest Tenor
[Image: Count John McCormack]

In the early 1890s, Michael Kilkelly, Athlone’s esteemed choirmaster, discovered young John McCormack. By 1902, McCormack’s voice, shaped by Kilkelly’s guidance, earned him a gold medal at the Dublin Feis Ceoil, launching him toward international stardom.

By the late 1910s, McCormack had risen to become the world’s most celebrated tenor. In America, his concerts filled halls, and his recordings sold millions, making him one of radio’s first major stars. In 1919, he became a U.S. citizen. His tours raised funds for charities, earning him the title of Papal Count in 1928. His performances carried Athlone’s spirit to global stages.

In June 1932, McCormack performed César Franck’s *Panis Angelicus* at the Eucharistic Congress in Dublin, captivating an estimated one million attendees. His voice, broadcast through Athlone’s new radio transmission station at Moydrum, reached homes across Ireland and Europe, uniting the nation following the Civil War’s divisions.

Athlone Pipers & Notable Stories

Cultural Defiance, Survival & Community Spirit
[Image: Athlone Pipers / Titanic / Fleadh]

Athlone Pipers Rebel: In the early 20th century, as the Gaelic League ignited a revival of Irish culture, Athlone’s uilleann pipers became vibrant symbols of rebellion. They revitalized the “pipes of the elbow,” performing at local feiseanna and community gatherings, their evocative reels and airs kindling pride in Irish identity. Their music strengthened the town’s resolve, ensuring that Ireland’s traditions thrived.

Survival at Sea (Eugene Daly & Bertha Mulvihill): In April 1912, Athlone piper Eugene Daly and Coosan's Bertha Mulvihill boarded the Titanic. Eugene played “A Nation Once Again” as the ship departed. When disaster struck, Eugene guided Bertha and his cousin to safety in Lifeboat 15, while he himself survived by clinging to an overturned collapsible boat. Their bravery and survival cemented their place in Athlone’s storied legacy.

Silence Falls & A New Beginning: The Irish Civil War (1922–1923) quieted Athlone's music scene. However, by 1926, Moydrum became home to Ireland’s first national radio station, 2RN (later Radio Éireann in 1938), broadcasting traditional Irish music and sparking a nationwide musical revival. This, along with the construction of the new Church of Saints Peter and Paul (begun 1932), symbolized Athlone's resilience.

Hosting the Fleadh: On May 24–25, 1953, Athlone hosted the third Fleadh Cheoil na hÉireann, drawing over 200 musicians and revitalizing its musical heritage. The event showcased talent and strengthened community ties.
